3C Work Observation ‚Äî Process Flow
Framework: Characterize ‚Üí Content ‚Üí Category. Purpose: expose waste and improvement opportunities
3C Framework 3C Work Observation ¬∑ End-to-End Flow
P
Pre-Study Preparation
Establish Baseline Before Entering the Gemba
Confirm Takt Time for the cell. Calculate Target Cycle Time (typically 85‚Äì95% of Takt). Know your required output (parts per shift/hour). Brief the operator ‚Äî never observe without notifying them; this is not surveillance, it is improvement. Prepare your video device: charge it, clean the lens, test the angle. The best angle captures the operator's hands and the workpiece simultaneously.
‚ö° Ohno Rule: Stand and observe for 60 minutes before touching anything. Your eyes must be calibrated first.

Select üìã 3C Work Observation. Fill all header fields before loading video.
Process / Station ‚òÖ Operator ‚òÖ Analyst ‚òÖ Takt Time (sec) ‚òÖ Target Cycle (sec) ‚òÖ Date ‚òÖ Department Team Leader Supervisor Part # / Description Required Quantity
üí° Tip: Takt Time = Available Time √∑ Customer Demand. If customer wants 100 units in 360 min available, Takt = 216 sec.
2
Video Load
Load Process Video ‚Üí Verify Playback
Tap Choose Video. Select the video from Camera Roll or Files. Wait for the video to render in the player. Scrub to the first step start point manually. Do not tap Start yet. Verify you can see the operator's hands clearly. If not ‚Äî your observation angle was wrong. Re-shoot before wasting analysis time.
‚úì Good angle: hands + workpiece visible throughout ‚úó Bad angle: operator's body blocks key motion
3
Capture ‚Äî Per Step
‚ñ∂ Start ‚Üí Operator Works ‚Üí ‚óº Step End
Tap ‚ñ∂ Start. Video plays. Watch carefully. The moment the work step changes ‚Äî tap ‚óº Step End. Video pauses. Fill the capture sheet:

Work Step name: Use verb + noun. "Apply adhesive to frame" not "adhesive." Be specific enough that someone who wasn't there would understand it.

ERACS: Assign one letter only ‚Äî Eliminate, Rearrange, Add/Subtract, Combine, Simplify. This is your improvement hypothesis, not a finding.

Work Content (pick ONE ‚Äî mutually exclusive):
‚úì Value Creating ‚Äî customer pays for this, product changes ‚ö° Incidental ‚Äî necessary but customer doesn't pay for it (get, put, walk) ‚úó Waste ‚Äî eliminate it: waiting, searching, rework, overproduction
Characteristics (mark all that apply ‚Äî these are observation flags, not classifications):
‚ö† Unsafe Work ‚Äî anything that creates injury risk ‚ö° Difficult Work ‚Äî ergonomic, precision, or coordination burden
Category (pick ONE):
C = Cyclical ‚Äî happens every cycle P = Periodic ‚Äî happens every N cycles A = Abnormal ‚Äî shouldn't happen, it's a defect in process
4
Timing Adjustment
Review Timestamps ‚Üí Adjust If Needed ‚Üí Save
Check the Video Start / End / Duration shown in the capture sheet. If you tapped Step End a second late (common), use ‚ü≤ -2s or ‚úì Set End Here to correct. Use ‚ñ∂Ô∏é Preview to watch just that step back before saving. Tap ‚úì Save & Continue. Video resumes from the corrected end point automatically.
üí° Pro tip: A 3C study should have 15‚Äì30 steps for most fenestration assembly stations. If you have fewer than 10, your step granularity is too coarse for meaningful ERACS analysis.
5
Multi-Cycle
‚óâ End Cycle ‚Üí Repeat 3‚Äì5 Cycles Minimum
When the operator completes one full unit, tap ‚óâ End Cycle. A new cycle tab appears. Repeat the study. Statistical validity requires a minimum of 3 cycles; 5 is preferred. Discard the first cycle ‚Äî operators adjust their pace when being observed (Hawthorne Effect). Use cycles 2‚Äì5 for analysis. The app will export all cycles.
‚úì 5 cycles = statistically defensible to management ‚úó 1 cycle = anecdote, not data
6
Export & Save
Export .xlsx ‚Üí Save JSON Backup ‚Üí Analyze
Tap ‚¨á Export .xlsx for the official template. Tap JSON to save your session state ‚Äî this is your backup and allows you to resume if interrupted. In Excel: the ERACS column and work content columns are pre-formatted. Check the totals row ‚Äî Value Creating % + Incidental % + Waste % should equal 100% of total time.
üí° Lean benchmark: World-class assembly operations achieve 70%+ Value Creating. If you see below 50%, you have a major opportunity ‚Äî and a story to tell in the supervisor meeting.

Job Instruction Sheet ‚Äî Process Flow
Purpose: define the quality standard and procedure for each step. The "what to check and how to check it" document.
JI Sheet Job Instruction Sheet ¬∑ End-to-End Flow
P
Pre-Study Preparation
Review Quality Standards Before You Film
Pull the current engineering drawing, quality spec, or customer requirement document. Know what pass/fail looks like for each step before you start. Identify where quality escapes have occurred historically ‚Äî those steps need the most rigorous criteria definition. Brief the operator: "I'm documenting how quality is built into this process, not auditing your performance."
‚ö° Key question: "What would cause this step to produce a defect that reaches the customer?" Answer that per step.
1
App Setup
Select Job Instruction ‚Üí Fill Header
Select üìÑ Job Instruction Sheet. JI exports include a quality-focused header.
Process / Station ‚òÖ Part # / Description ‚òÖ Required Quantity ‚òÖ Takt Time ‚òÖ Target Cycle Time ‚òÖ Date ‚òÖ Operator Team Leader Supervisor Analyst / Prepared By
2
Load Video
Load Video ‚Üí Position at Process Start
Load video as normal. For JI studies, you want a steady, well-lit shot that captures the quality characteristic being checked. If the quality check is a visual inspection of a seal or corner, the camera must be close enough to see it. A wide angle that captures motion is fine for 3C; JI needs detail shots of quality points.
3
Capture ‚Äî Per Step
‚ñ∂ Start ‚Üí Step End ‚Üí Fill Quality Fields
After tapping ‚óº Step End, fill the JI capture sheet:

Step Description: What the operator does. Active verb + object. "Apply perimeter sealant, 3mm bead, inside corner to corner."

Sampling Method: How often this quality check is performed.
100% ‚Äî check every unit (critical to safety or function) 1st PC ‚Äî check first piece of each run/shift Every Nth ‚Äî statistical sample (document N) Visual Audit ‚Äî periodic spot check
Acceptance Criteria: The specific, measurable definition of good. Not "looks good" ‚Äî that is not a criterion. Examples:
‚úì "Bead width 3mm ¬±0.5mm, no gaps >2mm" ‚úì "Label aligned within 2mm of corner mark, no bubbles" ‚úó "Sealant applied correctly" ‚Äî not measurable, not a standard
Notes / Special Instructions: Safety cautions, common error modes, tips that experience has revealed. This is tribal knowledge capture ‚Äî the stuff that only the best operators know.

Tools / Materials: List what is physically used in this step.
4
Review Cycle
Run 2‚Äì3 Cycles ¬∑ Focus on Quality Step Variation
JI studies benefit from observing 2‚Äì3 cycles specifically to see if the operator varies technique between cycles. Variation in a quality step is a red flag ‚Äî it means the standard isn't clear, isn't trained, or isn't achievable. Document it in Notes. If the operator does the step differently each time, you don't have a standard ‚Äî you have a guess.
üí° Rother: Variation in the method = variation in the output. Standardize the method before you try to improve the result.
5
Export & Validate
Export .xlsx ‚Üí Review with Quality / Engineering
Export the JI sheet. The output includes: step number, step description, sampling method, acceptance criteria, notes, tools, and timing. Do not publish this document without sign-off from Quality Engineering or the Process Engineer. The acceptance criteria must be technically validated before it becomes the standard. A JI sheet that ships with wrong criteria creates more defects than no JI sheet at all.
‚úì Signed off by: Quality Eng + Process Eng + Team Leader ‚úó Never: "We'll fix it later" ‚Äî an unchecked standard becomes permanent

Job Breakdown Sheet ‚Äî Process Flow
TWI Method: WHAT (important steps) ‚Üí HOW (key points) ‚Üí WHY (reasons). Purpose: operator training and knowledge transfer.
JB Sheet Job Breakdown Sheet ¬∑ End-to-End Flow
P
Pre-Study Preparation
Select Your Instructor ¬∑ Understand the TWI Model
Film your best operator ‚Äî not the most senior, the most consistent. The TWI method captures the technique that produces the best quality with the least effort. Understand the three-column structure before you start capturing:
WHAT = Important Step ‚Äî a logical part of the process where something happens to advance the work
HOW = Key Points ‚Äî anything that makes or breaks the job, injures the worker, or makes the work easier
WHY = Reasons ‚Äî why the key point matters (quality, safety, efficiency, customer)

The JB sheet is a training document, not a process audit. Write it so a new hire with zero background can perform the job correctly on their first supervised attempt.
1
App Setup
Select Job Breakdown ‚Üí Fill Header
Select üîß Job Breakdown Sheet. JB setup fields include Tools & Materials and PPE ‚Äî critical for training safety.
Process / Station ‚òÖ Tools & Materials ‚òÖ PPE Required ‚òÖ Operator (instructor) Department Date
‚ö° PPE: Select every applicable item. In fenestration, glass handling means Safety Gloves + Safety Sleeves + Safety Glasses as minimum. If it's not on the JB sheet, it's not part of the standard.
2
Strategy Choice
Decide: Direct JB Capture vs. Micro-Step + Group
You have two strategies ‚Äî choose before you start:

Strategy A ‚Äî Direct JB Capture: Capture steps at the WHAT level directly. Tap Step End when each important step completes. Fill in HOW, WHY, Safety in the capture sheet immediately. Best when you already know the job well.

Strategy B ‚Äî Micro-Steps First, Then Group: Capture at a very granular level (every small motion) for precise timing. Then use Select Steps ‚Üí Group as WHAT to combine micro-steps under WHAT headings. Best for complex jobs or when you want timing precision AND a training document.
‚úì Strategy A: You know the job. Faster. Less post-processing. üí° Strategy B: Complex job, new process, or need detailed timing data too. More work, richer output.
3
Capture ‚Äî Per Step
‚ñ∂ Start ‚Üí Step End ‚Üí Fill WHAT / HOW / WHY
After each ‚óº Step End:

Important Step (WHAT): Short, clear description. "Position sash in frame." "Apply corner key with mallet." Each WHAT should be a distinct phase of the work ‚Äî if you combine picking and placing and checking into one WHAT, you've lost the training value.

Key Points (HOW): This is the hardest column to write well. It must answer: what specifically does the operator feel, see, hear, or do that makes the difference between a good part and a bad part?
‚úì "Apply pressure at both corners simultaneously until audible click" ‚Äî specific, testable ‚úó "Press down firmly" ‚Äî not specific, not trainable
Reasons (WHY): Connect the key point to consequence. Operators who understand WHY maintain the standard without supervision. Operators who don't know WHY will take shortcuts the moment you leave.
‚úì "Both corners simultaneously prevents frame racking ‚Äî misalignment causes water infiltration failures in field"
Safety Notes: Step-specific hazards only. General PPE goes in the header.
4
Grouping (Strategy B Only)
Select Steps ‚Üí Group as WHAT ‚Üí Review
If using micro-step capture: tap Select Steps in the toolbar. Tap each micro-step that belongs to the same WHAT group. Tap Group as WHAT‚Ä¶ and enter the WHAT label. The export will combine HOW/WHY/Safety content and sum durations for the group. Review the grouped steps in the list to confirm they read correctly before exporting.
üí° Minimum 2 steps required to group. You can have ungrouped micro-steps alongside grouped ones ‚Äî the export handles both.
5
Validate
Apply the "New Hire Test" Before Exporting
Read your JB sheet aloud, imagining you are explaining it to someone on their first day. If any of these are true, revise before exporting:
‚úó A key point says "carefully" or "properly" without defining what that means
‚úó A WHY says "quality" without specifying which quality characteristic or what failure it prevents
‚úó A WHAT has no key points ‚Äî every important step has at least one thing that makes or breaks it
‚úì After reading it, a new hire would know exactly what to do and would understand why it matters
6
Export & Deploy
Export .xlsx ‚Üí Post at Workstation ‚Üí Use for Training
Export the JB sheet. The output is formatted in the classic TWI Job Breakdown layout: alternating row colors, STEP | WHAT | HOW | WHY columns, full header with PPE and tools. Print it. Laminate it. Post it at the workstation ‚Äî a JB sheet that lives only in a SharePoint folder is not standard work, it is a filing exercise.
‚úì Physical copy at workstation: operators can reference it during training ‚úì Used for structured TWI 4-step training: Prepare / Present / Try-out / Follow-up ‚úó Digital-only in a folder nobody opens: waste of your time

Common Mistakes ‚Äî All Study Types
Things that will get your study challenged in a supervisor review
‚ö† Timing Errors
Never start the clock at the wrong point in the cycle. Capture the full cycle ‚Äî from first touch to last touch, including all incidental work. Analysts who only time the "interesting" value-added steps produce studies that undercount by 30‚Äì40% and make the job look easier than it is.
‚ö† One-Cycle Studies
A single-cycle study is not a time study. It is a time observation. The difference matters to management, to engineering, and to any IE who reviews your work. Minimum 3 cycles. Discard cycle 1. Use the average of remaining cycles. Flag and explain any outlier cycle.
‚ö† Vague Step Names
Step names like "Assembly" or "Check" are not steps ‚Äî they are categories. A proper step name includes a verb + specific object. "Install corner key in lower-left sash corner" is a step. If two people would interpret your step name differently, it's not specific enough.
‚ö† Ignoring Abnormal Work
The most dangerous thing you can do is study only normal production. If material shortages, quality escapes, and equipment adjustments happen regularly, they belong in the study as Abnormal (A) category steps. These often represent your biggest cycle time variance and your biggest improvement opportunity.
Without standards, there can be no kaizen. You cannot improve what you have not defined. The Job Breakdown Sheet is not bureaucracy ‚Äî it is the baseline from which all improvement is measured.
